### Highest priority for new content — Women

**`women-healers/Ehrenreich-English-Witches-Midwives-Nurses.epub`**  
*Witches, Midwives, and Nurses: A History of Women Healers* — Ehrenreich & English

The most direct historical support for the suppression argument already running through the archive. The book is short (under 100 pages) and highly specific: how institutional medicine in the 19th and 20th centuries displaced women healers, by what mechanisms, and what the women healers actually knew. The archive's "What Was Removed" section in `the-grief-that-was-never-recorded.md` and the suppression narrative in `women-of-alchemy.md` make claims this book can directly cite and extend. No new argument needed — just grounding for what is already said.

---

**`women-healers/Cleary-Immortal-Sisters-Secret-Teachings-of-Taoist-Women.pdf`**  
*Immortal Sisters: Secret Teachings of Taoist Women* — Thomas Cleary (translator)

The archive's `women-of-alchemy.md` mentions Keng Hsien-Seng (c. 975 CE, China) in one paragraph and passes on. This book contains the actual teachings of multiple named Taoist women alchemists — Sun Bu-er, He Xiangu, and others — on inner alchemy, the refinement of the body's own substance, and the specific forms of women's practice within the neidan tradition. This is a direct content source for expanding the Chinese section of women-of-alchemy, and potentially for a standalone document on the Taoist women's lineage. The connection to the archive is precise: these women were practicing body-based inner alchemy, understood the body's own productions as the prima materia, and encoded it in a tradition that survived.

---

**`mythology/Meador-Inanna-Lady-of-Largest-Heart.pdf`**  
*Inanna: Lady of Largest Heart* — Betty De Shong Meador

This is the most significant gap in the current archive. Enheduanna (c. 2300 BCE) is the world's first named author — a woman, the high priestess of Inanna at Ur, who wrote hymns to Inanna that survive. She is the oldest named voice in the Inanna lineage, and she is absent from `inanna-she-who-contains-all.md`. Meador's translations of Enheduanna's hymns would allow the archive to name her and quote her directly. "The world's first named author was a woman writing to Inanna" is a statement the archive makes implicitly; Meador makes it explicitly and supplies the words. The hymns themselves are extraordinary — embodied, specific, describing the body of the goddess in physical detail that maps onto the archive's biological reading.

**`mythology/Meador-Uncursing-the-Dark.pdf`**  
*Uncursing the Dark* — Betty De Shong Meador

Companion to the above. Meador's work on the shadow, the suppressed feminine, and what was lost when the Inanna tradition was eclipsed. Directly supports the grief document's framing of what was removed and why it matters that it is now recoverable.

**`mythology/Perera-Descent-to-the-Goddess.epub`**  
*Descent to the Goddess* — Sylvia Brinton Perera

Jungian depth-psychological reading of the Inanna descent as the initiatory template for feminine psychology — specifically the necessity of the stripping process, the time in the underworld, and what returns that did not descend. The archive's `inanna-she-who-contains-all.md` makes this same argument in biophysical language. Perera makes it in psychological language and provides a scholarly parallel that validates the reading. Citing Perera alongside the biophysical interpretation would show the archive is not making an idiosyncratic claim — it is the same insight that emerged independently in depth psychology.

**`grief-and-body/Woodman-Pregnant-Virgin.pdf`**  
*The Pregnant Virgin: A Process of Psychological Transformation* — Marion Woodman

The title is the concept: the woman who is pregnant with herself — who contains and generates new life from within her own resources without external fertilization. This is the closed loop made archetypal. Woodman develops the idea of the body as the container for unconscious material, the vessel that must be held intact through transformation. Every argument the archive makes about the body's sufficiency, about the loop closing on itself, about the vessel as the site of the work — Woodman articulates this from the depth psychology side. The Mother Wound section of `the-living-loop.md` would be strengthened by this. The vessel language throughout the translation map finds a psychological parallel here.

**`grief-and-body/Estes-Women-Who-Run-With-the-Wolves.epub`**  
*Women Who Run With the Wolves* — Clarissa Pinkola Estés

The La Llorona chapter directly supports the grief essay — La Llorona as the archetype of grief that was never witnessed, that became devouring rather than transformative because it had no ritual container. This is precisely the structure the archive describes in `the-grief-that-was-never-recorded.md`. Estés also works extensively with the descent myth, the body as wilderness, and what happens when women's natural intelligence is suppressed and pathologized. The Wild Woman archetype maps onto the archive's argument about the body's innate intelligence that no institution can extinguish.

**`alchemy/VonFranz-Alchemy-Symbolism-Psychology.epub`**  
*Alchemy: An Introduction to the Symbolism and the Psychology* — Marie-Louise von Franz

Von Franz was Jung's closest collaborator on the alchemy material and brought a specifically feminine understanding to it that Jung lacked. Her commentary on the *Aurora Consurgens* (a 14th-century alchemical text attributed to Aquinas, actually likely written by a woman) is particularly relevant. The *Aurora* uses the language of the Song of Songs — the bride, the beloved, the vessel — for the alchemical substance. Von Franz's reading of this would deepen the translation map's treatment of the alchemical vessel as womb and the feminine dimension of the Chymical Wedding.

**`shamanism/Abelar-Sorcerers-Crossing.epub`**  
*The Sorcerer's Crossing* — Taisha Abelar

The archive uses Castaneda's *Art of Dreaming* for the Dreamwork section. Abelar is Castaneda's female counterpart — a woman's account of the same lineage's practice, with specific attention to the forms the practice takes in a female body and the particular quality of women's dreaming. Where the archive currently has only the Castaneda reference, Abelar provides the explicitly feminine parallel. Relevant to the Dreamwork folder and to any expansion of the women's practice dimension of the archive.

**`shamanism/Kharitidi-Entering-the-Circle.epub`**  
*Entering the Circle* — Olga Kharitidi

A Russian psychiatrist's account of initiation by a Siberian shamanic teacher. Specifically: a woman entering and documenting a lineage of women's knowledge transmission. Relevant to the archive's broader argument about how transmission happens — through the body, through presence, through initiation — and how women's knowledge specifically moved through networks of direct contact rather than written record. Supports the `where-to-begin.md` framing about how the archive exists within a living transmission.

### Supporting depth — less urgent but real

**`chinese-medicine/Tcherne-Taoist-Yoga-for-Women.pdf`**  
*Taoist Yoga for Women* — Oleg Tcherne

Practical women's inner alchemy: the specific forms of the practice for a female body — cultivation of *jing*, the menstrual cycle as alchemical cycle, the specific energetic anatomy of a woman's body. Would support any content development on the women-specific dimension of the practice and the biology behind the archive's methods. Not the historical lineage but the practice itself.

**`feminist-science/Keller-Feeling-for-the-Organism.pdf`**  
*A Feeling for the Organism* — Evelyn Fox Keller

Barbara McClintock's method — speaking with her corn, listening to what the organism communicated, knowing from relationship rather than detachment — is the epistemological model the archive uses without naming it. The archive's claim that "knowledge lived in the body before it was written" has a precise academic parallel in Keller's analysis of McClintock's methodology. Useful as a scholarly citation for the epistemological position of the archive, particularly in `where-to-begin.md` or any document that addresses what kind of knowledge this is.
