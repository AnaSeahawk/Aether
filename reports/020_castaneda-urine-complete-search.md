## Method

A plain-text OCR file containing all published Castaneda books in one document was located and downloaded from Internet Archive (`carlos-castaneda-all-books-in-one` and `carlos-castaneda-all-books-in-one-complete_202311`). Both versions were searched computationally for all word-forms of: `urine`, `urinate`, `urinating`, `urination`, `piss`, `pissed`, `pissing`, `pee`, `peeing`. 

Total corpus: ~5.8 million characters. Total specific matches: 26-28 (excluding false positives on "during", "curious", etc.). Every match was read in context. Book boundaries were identified from embedded title headers.

Taisha Abelar's *The Sorcerer's Crossing* epub was also searched computationally in full.

## Complete Passage Inventory by Book

### Book 1: *The Teachings of Don Juan* (1968)

Three urine-related passages found. None beyond the known dog incident involve teaching, prescription, or medicinal use.

**Passage 1-A** (pos ~35311): Incidental. Don Juan "went around the house to the chaparral to urinate." No teaching content; purely physiological narrative detail. Castaneda uses this moment to note he had a few minutes alone to think.

**Passage 1-B** (pos ~365339): Incidental. Castaneda hears sounds from inside and thinks don Juan "might have left through the back to urinate in the bushes." The sound is actually something else — a test or encounter. No urine teaching.

**Passages 1-C through 1-G** (pos ~72795-74131): The known peyote-night dog incident. Full text:

> "We left you here on the porch because we were afraid you were going to piss all over the rooms."
> "You pissed all over my dog!"
> "The dog was running because you were pissing on him."
> "My dog got even though; he pissed on you too!"
> "I swear my dog really pissed on you."
> Castaneda to don Juan: "But is this business of the dog and me pissing on each other true?"
> Don Juan: "It was not a dog! How many times do I have to tell you that? It was 'he' who played with you."

Don Juan's interpretation is that Mescalito (the peyote spirit) played with Castaneda and the dog was the form Mescalito took. The urination is incidental physical behavior during trance — Castaneda was unconscious of it at the time. Don Juan does not comment on the urine itself as significant; his sole comment is that Mescalito "liked" Castaneda.

**Assessment for Book 1:** No passage in this book reads as a teaching, prescription, or medicinal reference to urine. The user's memory that "the first book contains something that is then forgotten in all the rest" cannot be confirmed from this text. The peyote-dog incident is the only notable passage, and it does not contain a teaching about urine.

### Book 5: *The Second Ring of Power* (1977) — SIGNIFICANT FIND

This book contains the most substantive urine passage in the entire Castaneda corpus. The passage occurs in Chapter 5 of the book, which Castaneda himself titles "The Art of Dreaming" within *The Second Ring of Power*.

**Passage 5-A** — la Gorda, "Piss in your hands" (pos ~2309568 in the complete collection):

Context: Castaneda and la Gorda are moving through boulders at night. They sense a large, breathing entity approaching them in the dark.

> La Gorda was startled. She squatted and pulled me down with her by the shawl tied around my waist. She put her hands under her skirt for a moment and then stood up; her hands were clasped and when she snapped her fingers open a volley of sparks flew from them.
>
> "Piss in your hands," la Gorda whispered through clenched teeth.
>
> "Hub?" I said, unable to comprehend what she wanted me to do.
>
> She whispered her order three or four times with increasing urgency. She must have realized I did not know what she wanted, for she squatted again and showed that she was urinating in her hands. I stared at her dumbfounded as she made her urine fly like reddish sparks.
>
> My mind went blank. I did not know which was more absorbing, the sight la Gorda was creating with her urine, or the wheezing of the approaching entity. I could not decide on which of the two stimuli to focus my attention; both were enthralling.
>
> "Quickly! Do it in your hands!" la Gorda grumbled between her teeth.
>
> I heard her, but my attention was dislocated. With an imploring voice la Gorda added that my sparks would make the approaching creature, whatever it was, retreat. She began to whine and I began to feel desperate. I could not only hear but I could sense with my whole body the approaching entity. I tried to urinate in my hands; my effort was useless. I was too self-conscious and nervous. I became possessed by la Gorda's agitation and struggled desperately to urinate. I finally did it. I snapped my fingers three or four times, but nothing flew out of them.
>
> "Do it again," la Gorda said. "It takes a while to make sparks."
>
> I told her that I had used up all the urine I had. There was the most intense look of despair in her eyes.

The passage continues: the entity stops and they eventually escape by la Gorda standing on Castaneda's shoulders. The urine-as-sparks technique fails (or Castaneda cannot produce sparks), but the technique itself is real within the narrative — la Gorda uses it successfully. Her urine "flew like reddish sparks" from her cupped hands.

**What this passage says about urine:** La Gorda treats urine as an energetic weapon against non-ordinary entities. She knows the technique without instruction — she demonstrates it immediately under threat. The urine, when collected in cupped hands and snapped outward, produces "reddish sparks" that repel the entity. There is no explanation of mechanism; it is assumed knowledge. Castaneda cannot replicate the sparks even when he successfully urinates into his hands.

**What this passage does NOT say:** La Gorda does not explain the principle. Don Juan is not present. No one teaches the technique to Castaneda; it is shown under duress. It is not connected explicitly to healing or medicine. It is defensive sorcery — protection against a stalking entity.

**Assessment:** This is the closest passage in all of Castaneda to urine as an active substance with energetic properties. It is presented as received knowledge (la Gorda knows it; Castaneda does not). The connection to sovereign biophysics or endogenous distillation is inferential, not stated — the passage is about external defensive use (throwing urine outward as sparks), not internal use. But the idea that urine carries energetic charge that can be seen as light/sparks is textually present.

## Summary of Findings

**What IS in the corpus:**

1. **The peyote-dog incident** (Book 1, 1968): Already known. Involuntary urination during peyote trance. Don Juan interprets the mutual urination as Mescalito "playing with" Castaneda.

2. **La Gorda's "piss in your hands" technique** (Book 5, *The Second Ring of Power*, 1977): The most energetically significant passage. La Gorda demonstrates urine collected in cupped hands and snapped outward as "reddish sparks" that repel an approaching entity. This is the only passage in the corpus where urine is treated as having an intrinsic energetic property. Castaneda fails to replicate the sparks. The technique is assumed knowledge (la Gorda does not explain it). It is not healing or internal use — it is defensive external projection.

3. **Theresa revives Alex** (Book 10, *The Active Side of Infinity*, 1998): Incidental anecdote about urine as emergency resuscitation. Don Juan uses the story for a teaching about self-reflection, not about urine.

**What is NOT in the corpus:**

- No passage where don Juan prescribes urine as medicine
- No passage where urine appears in a Datura or peyote preparation
- No passage where any character drinks, collects, or uses urine internally
- No passage where don Juan comments on the properties of urine
- No passage explicitly connecting urine to healing, strength, or clarity
- No passage from Book 1 beyond the peyote dog incident

## Assessment of the User's Memory

The user stated: "in the very first Carlos Castaneda book he actually mentions urine... then it's forgotten in all the rest of the books."

After exhaustive search: this does not match any passage found. The first book contains three urine references — two incidental (don Juan urinating outside) and one the peyote-dog incident. None reads as a teaching. The peyote-dog incident is not "forgotten" in later books — it is referenced again in Book 2.

The passage that most resembles what the user describes — urine as an energetic substance with a visible property — is in **Book 5, not Book 1**, and it features la Gorda, not don Juan. It is also not a prescription but an emergency technique. It does not appear again in later books.

**Two possibilities:**

1. The user may be misremembering *which* book the passage is from, and may be thinking of the la Gorda passage from *The Second Ring of Power* (1977). This would make it "from the middle of the series" not "the first book," but the la Gorda passage is distinctive enough that it could be the memory anchor.

2. The user may have encountered an edition, translation, or commentary that includes a passage not in these OCR texts. The OCR quality of these scans is imperfect in places. However, the scans are sufficiently legible that a substantial teaching passage would not be missed.

**The la Gorda passage is recommended for the SB archive** as the most relevant Castaneda corpus citation for the principle that urine carries an energetic charge visible under certain conditions of perception (it appears as "reddish sparks" when la Gorda uses it). Whether that constitutes a "teaching" about urine or only demonstrates it is a question the text leaves open.
