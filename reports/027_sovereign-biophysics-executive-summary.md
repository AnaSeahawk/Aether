## IV. Cross-Tradition Coherence Check

### Terminology Consistency

**Generally consistent.** The triple-register vocabulary (alchemical/Ayurvedic/biophysical) is used correctly and consistently throughout. The Translation Map (10-method/sovereign-biophysics-translation-map.md) is doing real work as the architectural key.

**One potential confusion point:** The word *soma* is used in two distinct senses:
1. Vedic ritual: soma as the pressed, filtered, golden drink from an external plant source (the *Rigveda* context)
2. Endogenous: soma as the cultivated nectar produced by the body through practice (Frawley's Soma in Yoga and Ayurveda usage)

These are not the same thing, and the archive moves between them. Frawley's argument (which the archive follows) is that the endogenous soma was the original and the plant ritual a later exoteric substitute — but this is Frawley's interpretive position, not established scholarship. A brief note acknowledging this distinction and explaining the archive's usage would prevent a reader from stopping on what looks like equivocation.

**The agni-soma pair:** Consistently handled. The Rigvedic pairing (solar/fire/masculine × lunar/water/feminine) correctly mapped onto Solar spirit and Lunar essence. No confusion here.

### Ayurvedic Frame

Coherent across the archive. The dhatvagni chain (rasa → rakta → mamsa → meda → asthi → majja → shukra/artava → ojas) is introduced in agni.md and carried correctly through the synthesis documents. The four states of agni (sama, vishama, tikshna, manda) are introduced and applied to actual current observation in the May 2026 current state — this is textbook-quality integration of framework with lived observation.

**One gap:** The archive doesn't draw on the Ayurvedic concept of *anupana* (the vehicle substance that carries another medicine deeper into the tissues). This is directly relevant to how the Lunar essence is being used as a carrier in food and drink — the essence is functioning as anupana for the herbs, the grain, everything taken with it. Frawley & Lad cover anupana in *Yoga of Herbs* and the Charaka Samhita has a complete chapter on it. This would give the dietary practice a more precise Ayurvedic vocabulary.

### Chinese Medicine and Taoism

**Coherently handled** in Sun Bu-er and the Distilled Fraction Protocols. The jing/qi/shen progression (essence → vital force → spirit) maps correctly onto the three fractions. The Triple Burner fractionation (Lower Burner → Lunar essence, Middle Burner → Qi) is documented in the Cross-Traditional Analysis.

**What's absent:** The *Energetic Alchemy* material from Chinese Medical Qigong Vol. 2 — the specific practices by which the body is modified to produce better medicine. This is the book you asked about. The text describes the Taoist/neidan approach to body cultivation: practices that deliberately transform jing (the raw essence at the kidney level) into qi (the vital force), and qi into shen (spirit). The alchemical language for this is *Embryonic Breathing*, the Microcosmic Orbit, and specific methods for ascending and recirculating essence rather than expending it. These practices change what the body produces in its Lower Burner — and therefore what appears in the distillate.

**The essay that's missing:** "How to Make the Medicine" — an integration of:
- Chinese Medical Qigong Vol. 2 on Energetic Alchemy and the transformation of jing through practice
- The Hatha Yoga Pradipika on khechari mudra, pranayama, and the descent of amrita from the crown

This essay would be the most practically useful document in the archive for a practitioner trying to understand *why the diet and practices matter to the quality of the medicine* — not just that they do, but how.

### Buddhist Connections

The Pali Canon citation (pūtimuttabhesajja) is correctly documented with volume and translation. The Vajrayana connection (amrta, kalachakra, Tibetan inner heat practices) is present but not developed.
