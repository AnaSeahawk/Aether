## Vol. 1 — Energetic Anatomy and Physiology
**Chapters 1–10 | Section I: Foundations of Chinese Energetic Medicine**

The opening volume builds the complete ontological and anatomical ground on which all subsequent clinical work stands. It makes no assumptions — Johnson begins before the body exists, with the cosmological sequence from Dao → Wuji → Taiji → Yin/Yang → Five Elements, treating this not as background philosophy but as the actual generative process that produces physiological structure.

### Chapter-by-Chapter

- **Ch. 1** — History and definition of Medical Qigong: its origin in shamanic Wu Yi medicine, development through the Imperial College, the three main schools (Daoist, Buddhist, Confucian), and how CMQigong sits alongside acupuncture and herbology within TCM.
- **Ch. 2** — Ancient Chinese metaphysics in depth: the soul anatomy (Shen Ling, Shen Xian, Yuan Shen, Shen Zhi), the Three Bodies (physical, energetic, spiritual), Daoist embryology across the ten lunar months of gestation, the prenatal/postnatal split in constitutional patterning, and the Hun (three ethereal souls) and Po (seven corporeal souls) — including how the soul exits at death.
- **Ch. 3** — Tissue formation: fascial development as an energetic medium, the five constitutional body types (Wood, Fire, Earth, Metal, Water) with their physical and psychological signatures, and congenital vs. acquired disorders.
- **Ch. 4** — The five energies of the human body (Yuan Qi, Zong Qi, Ying Qi, Wei Qi, and mixed forms).
- **Ch. 5** — The Taiji Pole and Three Dantians: the vertical central channel, the functional fields of each dan tian, and how they relate to Jing, Qi, and Shen respectively.
- **Ch. 6** — The Eight Extraordinary Vessels: their role as the body's deep reservoir system that stores overflow and compensates deficiency across the primary channels.
- **Ch. 7** — The Six Extraordinary Organs (brain, marrow, bone, vessels, gallbladder, uterus).
- **Ch. 8** — The Twelve Primary Organs, Channels, and Collaterals: the most extensive chapter, covering each organ network's physiology, psychological function, and channel pathway in detail.
- **Ch. 9** — The secondary channel systems: divergent channels, sinew channels, luo vessels, and skin zones.
- **Ch. 10** — Acupoints and their clinical classification.

### Defining Move
Johnson insists that the energetic anatomy is also a psychological and spiritual anatomy — every meridian carries emotional memory, every organ network holds a specific archetypal psycho-spiritual function. Pathology is never only physical.

## Vol. 2 — Energetic Alchemy, Dao Yin Therapy, and Qi Deviations
**Chapters 11–20 | Sections II–IV**

Where Vol. 1 maps what the body is, Vol. 2 addresses how it transforms — and what happens when transformation goes wrong. It is simultaneously a treatise on internal alchemy, a training manual for Dao Yin (therapeutic movement/breath/mind cultivation), and a clinical guide to Qi deviation disorders.

### Section II — Medical Qigong and Chinese Alchemy (Chs. 11–15)

- **Ch. 11** — Internal/External alchemy, the Dao, the Nine Treasures, the Three Treasures (Jing/Qi/Shen) and their correspondence to Heaven/Earth/Man Qi. Includes Ming Shu (fate calculation) and the Four Pillars of Destiny — Johnson treats astrological timing as clinically relevant.
- **Ch. 12** — The Three Treasures of Heaven: Sun, Moon, and Stars. Detailed treatment of how celestial cycles (daily, lunar, seasonal, stellar) directly shape the body's Qi patterns. Includes the Twelve Pi Hexagrams, the Twenty-Eight Star Constellations, and practical guidance on timing Qigong practice relative to these cycles.
- **Ch. 13** — The Three Treasures of Earth: Soil, Water, and Wind. How to gather Qi from natural environments — mountains, caves, trees, oceans, rivers. Covers food as Earth treasure, herbal Qi, and the energetic qualities of different directions of wind.
- **Chs. 14–15** — The Three Treasures of Man: the practitioner's own Jing, Qi, and Shen as the instrument of clinical work, and the alchemical process of internal refinement (Lian Jing Hua Qi → Lian Qi Hua Shen → Lian Shen Huan Xu).

### Section III — Dao Yin Therapy (Chs. 16–19)

- **Ch. 16** — Postural Dao Yin training: the physical layer of self-cultivation and therapeutic movement.
- **Ch. 18** — Mental Dao Yin training: visualization, Yi (intention) development, and meditation methods as clinical tools.
- Intervening and following chapters cover breathing and vocal/sound Dao Yin.

### Section IV — Qi Deviations (Ch. 20)

- **Ch. 20** — Disorders of the Soul and Spirit: what happens when the cultivation process generates Qi deviations — energy that goes wrong in direction, intensity, or quality. Covers symptom presentations and corrections.

### Defining Move
Vol. 2 is the one that most directly addresses the practitioner's own development. The cosmological scope (Sun/Moon/Stars/Earth/Wind/Water) is not academic — it is the energetic environment the practitioner and patient inhabit, and knowing how to work with it is as clinical as knowing how to diagnose.

## Vol. 3 — Differential Diagnosis
**Chapters 21–39 | Sections V–VIII**

The clinical pivot of the series. This is where theory becomes assessment, and assessment becomes intervention. Vol. 3 is substantially longer in scope than the previous two — it covers not just diagnosis (Chs. 21–26) but the full range of clinical treatment techniques (Chs. 27–39), making it the operational core of the textbook series.

### Section V — Differential Diagnosis (Chs. 21–26)

- **Ch. 21** — Introduction to differential diagnosis: etiology across three life stages (womb, childhood, adulthood), the complete diagnostic sequence from intake through treatment planning.
- **Ch. 22** — Diagnosis by Eight Energetic Principles: Yin/Yang, Interior/Exterior, Cold/Hot, Deficient/Excess — with specific pulse positions and qualities for each, and Yin/Yang constitution charts.
- **Ch. 23** — Diagnosis by Qi, Blood, and Body Fluid dysfunction: deficient Qi, excess Qi, Qi stagnation, rebellious Qi, turbid Qi, Blood deficiency, Blood stasis, Blood heat/cold, phlegm in all its forms (substantial and non-substantial).
- **Ch. 24** — Five Element diagnosis: each element's full diagnostic signature, Sheng/Ke cycle pathologies (over-acting and insulting).
- **Ch. 25** — Six Stages (Shang Han) and Four Levels (Wen Bing): the classical febrile disease progression models applied to energetic diagnosis.
- **Ch. 26** — Full clinical examination: pulse reading, tongue, visual field assessment, intake forms, patient symptom charting.

### Section VI — Clinical Setup (Chs. 27–30)

- **Ch. 27** — Materializing and dematerializing energy: the mechanics of how a practitioner's Yi and Shen can condense or dissolve Qi in a patient's field.
- **Ch. 28** — Establishing the Medical Qigong clinic: physical setup, energetic preparation of the space, protecting the treatment room.
- **Ch. 29** — Combining CMQigong with acupuncture, herbology, massage, and Western medicine.
- **Ch. 30** — Precautions and ethics: contraindications, patient protection, the practitioner's responsibilities.

### Section VII — Treatment Principles (Chs. 31–34)

- **Ch. 31** — Principles and parameters of therapy.
- **Ch. 32** — Balancing the body's energetic system: tonification, sedation, regulation.
- **Ch. 33** — The Magic Square of the Yellow River (Lo Shu): its application in clinical spatial and temporal Qi balancing.
- **Ch. 34** — Healing the patient's emotional traumas: the clinical treatment of the seven emotions as pathogenic forces, methods for releasing emotional Qi.

### Section VIII — Emission Techniques (Chs. 35–39)

- **Ch. 35** — Energy extension and Qi manipulation: hand techniques, field extension, basic emission methods.
- **Ch. 36** — Advanced Qi emission: vibration projection, long-distance emission, Shen-to-Shen transmission.
- **Ch. 37** — Invisible Needle theory and application: using projected Qi to stimulate acupoints without physical contact.
- **Ch. 38** — Medical Qigong massage and tissue regulation.
- **Ch. 39** — Sound and color projection: the theory of vibrational medicine and clinical application of the five element colors and healing sounds as direct Qi interventions.

### Defining Move
Vol. 3 refuses to separate diagnosis from treatment — the same perceptual capacities used to read a patient (field scanning, Shen sensitivity) are the same ones used in the advanced emission techniques. Diagnosis and treatment are continuous acts.

## Vol. 4 — Prescription Exercises
**Chapters 40–55 | Sections IX–XI**

The practical reference volume. Organized by modality and then by organ system and clinical population. This is the book a practitioner picks up to answer the question: "I've diagnosed this pattern — what do I prescribe?"

### Section IX — Core Prescriptions (Chs. 40–44)

- **Ch. 40** — Introduction: the history of Dao Yin exercises, the three foundational training methods (physical/breath/mind), the five approaches to self-regulation, three categories of prescription exercises (quiescent, dynamic, combined), how to choose postures and calculate breath counts using Yin/Yang and Five Element respiratory ratios.
- **Ch. 41** — Internal organ self-massage: detailed step-by-step protocols for each of the five Zang organs (Liver, Heart, Spleen, Lung, Kidney). Each follows the same structure: bone stacking → organ regulation → point respiration → Pulling Down the Heavens → Opening/Closing the Lower Dan Tian. Includes a prescription for insomnia.
- **Ch. 42** — Healing sound prescriptions for purgation: the five healing sounds with precise pitch, volume, and breath mechanics. High/middle/low pitch distinctions, Yin/Yang breath separation.
- **Ch. 43** — Prescription exercises and meditations: the core general library of CMQigong exercises matched to diagnostic categories.
- **Ch. 44** — Daoist Medical Qigong prescription exercises: exercises from the Daoist tradition with clinical applications.

### Section X — Qi Emission Therapy by Organ (Chs. 45–50)

Practitioner-directed treatment protocols for each organ network and miscellaneous conditions — covering etiology, treatment principle, specific emission techniques, hand positions, and point selections for each organ system's common disease patterns.

- Ch. 45 — Liver diseases
- Ch. 46 — Heart diseases
- Ch. 47 — Spleen and Stomach diseases
- Ch. 48 — Lung diseases
- Ch. 49 — Kidney diseases
- Ch. 50 — Miscellaneous diseases

### Section XI — Specialty Populations (Chs. 51–55)

- **Ch. 51** — Pediatrics: modified protocols for children, with attention to developmental energetic stages and common pediatric patterns.
- **Ch. 52** — Geriatrics: treatment considerations for Jing depletion, constitutional decline, and the specific patterns of aging.
- **Ch. 53** — Gynecology: the largest specialty chapter — menstrual disorders, fertility, pregnancy, menopause, from an energetic medicine perspective.
- **Ch. 54** — Neurology: stroke, paralysis, tremor, and neurological conditions understood as specific meridian, channel, and Shen disruptions.
- **Ch. 55** — Psychology: mental-emotional disorders mapped to organ network imbalances and Shen disturbances, with specific Qigong prescriptions.

### Defining Move
The internal organ self-massage protocols in Ch. 41 are the patient self-care backbone of the whole clinical system — designed to be assigned as homework, they give the patient active agency in their own treatment and account for a significant portion of therapeutic outcome.

## Vol. 5 — Energetic Oncology
**Chapters 56–81 | Section XII: The Treatment of Cancer with Medical Qigong**

The most specialized and in some ways the most philosophically ambitious volume. Vol. 5 applies the complete CMQigong framework to cancer — and in doing so, articulates what the series has been building toward: a medicine capable of holding physical, energetic, emotional, ancestral, and spiritual dimensions of illness simultaneously.

### Ch. 56 — Foundation of Energetic Oncology
History of cancer treatment in China; the critical distinction between cysts, tumors, and cancers from both ancient and modern Chinese medicine perspectives; detailed tumor categorization; cancer metastasis and staging; and a multifactorial causal model that includes environmental, chemical, genetic, psychological, and energetic/biological factors. Cancer is understood as arising from Qi stagnation, toxic accumulation, Wei Qi collapse, and psychological/spiritual disruption interacting.

### Ch. 57 — Clinical Protocol Framework
The CMQigong approach to cancer treatment laid out in full: clinical treatment theory, the standard protocol structure, the range of modalities employed, and how to select prescriptions. Crucially: the chapter addresses the practitioner's own internal belief system as a variable in treatment outcomes.

### Chs. 58–74 — Cancer-Specific Protocols

One chapter per cancer type. Each follows a consistent structure:
1. Anatomy from both Western and Chinese medicine perspectives
2. Energetic etiology (which organ network imbalances and emotional patterns predispose)
3. Symptom profile
4. Multiple numbered treatment protocols (specifying Qi emission technique, point selections, color/sound projections)
5. Patient homework prescription exercises

### Chs. 75–76 — Integration with Biomedical Treatment

- **Ch. 75** — Working alongside radiation and chemotherapy: specific Qigong protocols to counteract radiation damage (heat toxin, tissue injury), manage chemotherapy side effects (nausea, immune suppression, fatigue), and support the patient's Zheng Qi through cytotoxic treatment.
- **Ch. 76** — Surgery support: pre- and post-surgical protocols, restoring Qi flow across disrupted meridian pathways, addressing scar tissue and Blood loss.

### Ch. 77 — Herbs and Nutrition
Integration of dietary and herbal support within the oncology framework.

### Ch. 78 — Social Oncology
The relational and community dimensions of cancer treatment — family support, social isolation as pathogenic factor, the role of group practice.

### Chs. 79–80 — Cancer Prescription Meditations and Exercises
The patient self-care library specific to oncology.

### Ch. 81 — Understanding Death and Dying
The final chapter of the entire series. Covers the Daoist understanding of death as transition, protocols for supporting a patient's Shen through the dying process, the energetic physiology of dying, and what the practitioner's role is when cure is no longer the goal. This chapter is rare in the clinical literature — it treats dying as a medical domain that requires its own competencies, not as treatment failure.

### Defining Move
The consistent structure across all 17 cancer-specific chapters means this volume is both philosophically coherent and immediately clinically usable. The final chapter on death and dying completes the series' arc: from the cosmological formation of the body in Vol. 1 to its dissolution in Vol. 5.
