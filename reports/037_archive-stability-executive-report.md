- private source chamber: The Vessel;

- `analysis-plasma-composition-hypothesis.md`
- `analysis-fraction-biochemistry.md`

Most other high-risk pages do not yet carry visible claim posture near the top.

This is the main live risk.

### Scientific Evidence Stability: Low-to-Medium

The archive is scientifically meaningful as hypothesis-generating work.

It is not yet a controlled scientific record.

Strong evidence:

- repeated field observations;
- measurement tables from specific distillation runs;
- instrument readings for pH, ORP, EC, TDS, temperature, and salinity estimates;
- internally consistent fraction behavior;
- external sources for known biochemical principles.

Weak or missing evidence:

- no GC/MS constituent assays;
- no hormone assays of fractions;
- no 24-hour urinalysis baseline;
- no microbial characterization of aging;
- no controlled comparison group;
- no formal instrument calibration log;
- limited standardization of sample collection conditions.

The scientific hole exists because the work began as lived observation, not as an institutional study. The archive has become rigorous after the fact. The older data is real, but not standardized to the level a laboratory reader would expect.

The symbolic map is coherent.

This is one of the archive's greatest strengths.

### Publication Safety: Medium

The public/private boundary is much stronger than it was.

The private Vessel exists as the protected source layer. The public archive acknowledges sensitive material without exposing it directly.

Remaining risk:

- public summaries must avoid becoming covert protocols;

The hole exists because the body record is part of the work, but not all true material belongs in public.

## Red Flags

### Red Flag 1: Strong Mechanistic Language Without Assays

Some pages speak with biochemical force.

That is acceptable when the statement is externally documented. It becomes risky when exact fraction composition is inferred without assays.

Protection:

- claim-posture blocks;
- careful language around inference;
- no universal composition claims;
- no medical or therapeutic promises.

### Red Flag 2: "Salt %" Can Be Misread

Meter salt percentage can be mistaken for sodium chloride proof.

The Measurement Legend now protects this, but older readers may still misread it.

Protection:

- continue linking the Measurement Legend from experiment reports;
- avoid saying "salt" without qualifying meter estimate vs mineral salts vs NaCl.

### Red Flag 3: "Plasma" Remains A Volatile Word

The archive uses plasma as an internal working term.

External readers may confuse it with blood plasma or assume a biomedical category.

Protection:

- continue linking `terms/plasma.md`;
- qualify the term in public-facing summaries;
- avoid using plasma alone when the external register is active.

### Red Flag 4: Practice Language Can Become Instruction

Some method pages are close to protocol.

Even with non-medical framing, a reader may imitate.

Protection:

- keep instructional status visible;
- add claim posture to protocol-adjacent pages;
- avoid directive language;
- keep intimate operational material private.

### Red Flag 5: Search Entrances Bypass The Map

A reader may land on a high-intensity page without seeing the frontispiece, Dictionary, or public/private boundary.

Protection:

- page-level claim posture;
- short boundary notes near the top of high-risk pages;
- consistent "Back to Dictionary" and "Back to Index" links.

### Red Flag 6: AI Drift

The archive has been shaped through AI-assisted work.

The risk is that the machine over-systematizes the living material, smooths the voice, or inflates coherence where the record is still open.

Protection:

- preserve field notes;
- keep uncertainty visible;
- do not mass-normalize prose;
- let new records pressure-test the map.

## Audience Read

### If I Am An Investigator

The archive is navigable and increasingly auditable.

The provenance structure is improving: public summaries, private sources, reports, and bibliography are distinguishable.

The next investigative need is source traceability. Which external source supports which claim? Which private record produced which public summary? Which observation has repeated enough to become a pattern?

### If I Am A Journalist

The story is strong.

The danger is sensational framing. The archive touches taboo material, women's hidden knowledge, alchemy, body fluids, sexuality, medicine, and institutional erasure. That is compelling and easy to distort.

The best journalistic frame is not "a miracle substance" or "ancient secret rediscovered."

The stronger frame is:

> A living archive is attempting to distinguish observation, measurement, myth, tradition, hypothesis, and private body record without forcing them into one false category.

### If I Am A Scientist

The archive is a hypothesis-generating field record.

It contains meaningful measurements, coherent questions, and testable directions.

It does not yet contain lab verification of the strongest biochemical inferences.

A scientist should ask:

- What exactly was measured?
- With what instrument?
- Under what conditions?
- What was inferred?
- What assay would confirm or falsify the inference?

The archive can answer some of these now. Others remain open.

### If I Am A Mystic

The archive is spiritually coherent because it no longer asks myth to impersonate measurement.

Myth, body, water, vessel, descent, return, charge, salt, and medicine are allowed to speak as symbolic and experiential truths.

The risk for the mystic is over-belief.

The archive's restraint is part of the sacred container.

Mystery is protected by not turning it into proof too soon.
