# 1. Stem Cells: Ontology and Artifact Map

**Date:** 2026-05-22  
**Agent:** Codex / GPT-5  
**Session topic:** First research map for whether stem cells are living entities, lab artifacts, or assay-defined states.

---

## Core Orientation

Stem cells are living cells, not inert residue, protein precipitate, or "ash" at the bottom of a dish. But "stemness" is not a visible substance inside the cell. It is an observed capacity: the cell can self-renew and can generate differentiated descendants under the right conditions.

The clean epistemic frame is:

- A stem cell is a real cell.
- Stemness is a behavior and potential, proven by assay.
- Isolation can alter the cell.
- Culture can select for cells that survive the dish rather than faithfully represent the living tissue.
- The strongest evidence comes from in vivo lineage tracing, clonal analysis, single-cell transplantation, and serial transplantation.

So the research question should not be "do stem cells exist?" The better question is: what assay proves that this particular claimed stem cell behaves as a stem cell in its native tissue, and how much did the isolation method change it?

## What Counts As Strong Evidence

Weak evidence:

- Marker expression alone, such as a surface protein or gene signature.
- A cell looking "stem-like" in a dish.
- A commercial preparation labelled "stem cells" without functional proof.

Moderate evidence:

- Clonal growth from one cell.
- Differentiation into more than one lineage in culture.
- Stable expression of known stem-cell-associated genes.

Strong evidence:

- In vivo lineage tracing: marked cells generate the expected tissue lineages inside a living animal.
- Single-cell transplantation: one purified cell can reconstitute a tissue system.
- Serial transplantation: descendants can reconstitute again, showing self-renewal rather than one-time proliferation.
- Spatial evidence showing the cell in its niche.

## Anchor Examples

Hematopoietic stem cells are the strongest historical case. Till and McCulloch's 1961 spleen-colony work created a functional assay for blood-forming cells. Becker, McCulloch, and Till then showed the clonal nature of spleen colonies in 1963. Later single-cell transplant studies showed that a single sorted hematopoietic stem cell could reconstitute blood lineages in recipient mice.

Intestinal stem cells are a strong in vivo case. Barker et al. used Lgr5 lineage tracing in adult mice and found that Lgr5-positive crypt-base cells generated intestinal epithelial lineages over a 60-day period. This is much stronger than dish morphology because the behavior was tracked in tissue.

Human embryonic stem cell lines and induced pluripotent stem cells are real living cell lines, but they are also culture-dependent states. Thomson et al. derived human embryonic stem cell lines from blastocysts in 1998. Takahashi and Yamanaka showed that defined factors could reprogram adult human fibroblasts into induced pluripotent stem cells in 2007. These are not "ash"; they are living, proliferating cell populations. But their truth is partly laboratory truth: they are maintained by recipes, media, surfaces, and selective pressure.

## Artifact Risks

The artifact concern is valid. The field itself documents it.

Cultured human embryonic stem cells can acquire genetic and epigenetic changes over passages. Maitra et al. found that late-passage hESC lines had genomic alterations relative to early-passage lines, including copy-number changes, mitochondrial DNA sequence changes, and promoter methylation changes.

Adult stem cells often depend on a niche. Schofield's 1978 niche hypothesis framed the stem cell as a cell whose behavior is determined by association with surrounding cells and microenvironment. Removing the cell can change the thing being studied.

Mesenchymal "stem cell" claims require special caution. The International Society for Cellular Therapy proposed minimal criteria for "multipotent mesenchymal stromal cells" in 2006, which already signals that many claims are operational and culture-defined rather than proof of a universal regenerative cell.

Commercial stem-cell therapy claims are a separate evidentiary category. The FDA warns that many regenerative medicine products marketed as stem cells or exosomes are unapproved and have not been shown safe or effective for many claimed uses.

## Research Path For Today

1. Start with definitions from NIH, but treat them as orientation, not proof.
2. Read the hematopoietic lineage first because it has the cleanest functional history.
3. Read an in vivo lineage-tracing case, especially Lgr5 intestinal stem cells.
4. Separate naturally resident adult stem cells from embryonic stem cell lines and iPSCs.
5. Build an "artifact audit" for every paper:
   - Was the cell observed in vivo or only after isolation?
   - Was it one cell, a sorted population, or a mixed preparation?
   - Was stemness proven by function or inferred from markers?
   - Did the result require culture conditions that may create or select the state?
   - Were long-term and serial assays performed?

## Working Conclusion

Stem cells are real and alive. The mistake is treating "stem cell" as a fixed essence rather than a living capacity held between cell, tissue, niche, and experimental condition.

The true form is closest in the living tissue. The dish is useful, but it must be questioned.
