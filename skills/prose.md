# Skill — prose

*Craft moves for writing that says true things in the voice of this work.*

---

## What this skill is for

Every piece of writing in this repo — essays, orientation entries, presentation
slides, companion notes — lives or dies on its sentences. This skill names the
concrete moves: how to open, how to close, what to delete, how to let a primary
source carry weight. It is the craft layer between `skills/writer.md` (the role)
and `soul.md` (the upstream voice).

---

## Voice — the first constraint

From `soul.md`: *quiet, precise, relational. Oriented to the body and the
moment. Refuses coercion, hype, or spectacle.*

Every craft decision flows from this. The voice is not a style applied after
the fact — it is the criterion by which a sentence is kept or cut.

**Declarative, present tense.** The prose stands in the voice of the finished
work. Hedges ("perhaps," "it could be argued," "one might say") are evidence the
claim isn't earned. Either earn it and state it, or trim the sentence to what the
evidence actually supports.

**Single-subject framing.** The writer records observation and experience. The
sentence does not instruct the reader's body. "I found that the heat moved
inward" — not "you will notice the heat moving inward."

---

## Begin with the substance

Drop the reader into the thing. No preamble, no announcement of what the piece
will do. The first sentence is part of the argument.

Delete on sight:

- "This essay explores..."
- "In what follows..."
- "Before we begin..."
- "It is worth noting that..."
- "Notice how..." / "Observe that..."

Instead: open inside the thing itself. If the piece is about agni, the first
sentence names something true about agni — not "agni is an important concept."

---

## Primary sources as load-bearing surface

When a primary source exists — a Caraka verse, an alchemical text, a clinical
study — let it carry the argument rather than speaking over it. The pattern:

1. One or two sentences of orientation.
2. The quote block.
3. A sentence naming what the quote established.
4. The next move.

The prose around the source is scaffolding. The source is the load-bearing
surface. Paraphrasing when the source is at hand concedes ground the source
already holds.

Sanskrit quote block form:

```markdown
> *Carakasaṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 1.15 (Sharma & Dash, Vol. 1)
>
> "The English translation."
```

Sanskrit name in italics, sthāna and adhyāya, translator in parentheses.
Sanskrit before English when both are given. Use IAST diacritics throughout
(ā, ī, ū, ṭ, ḍ, ṇ, ś, ṣ, ḥ, ṃ).

## Movement — particular to general

Open in a specific, felt thing. Rise into the larger observation. The reverse —
opening in abstraction and descending to example — leaves the reader guessing
what the abstraction is for until the example arrives.

If the piece is about receptivity as intelligence: open with the moment the body
stopped resisting, not with a definition of receptivity.

---

## Rhythm — short sentences, earned breath

Sentences cluster short. An aphoristic register sits inside the prose. Long
sentences are admitted when the length itself is the point — a catalogue that
needs to accumulate, a clause whose internal structure carries meaning. Not as
a default.

Em-dashes mark genuine asides or rhythmic breaks. A paragraph with more than
two em-dashes is usually restructurable. Keep density low.

---

## The closer

End on a brief declarative. The closer distils what the prose earned — it does
not summarize what the prose just said. Restatement flattens what was built.
Cut it.

Reference form: one short sentence that names the earned thing directly.
*"The heat was already there. It was waiting to be received."*

---

## What to delete on sight

**Negative-contrast constructions.** The signature pattern of LLM drafts:
building a positive claim by first denying an alternative.

- "X is not Y — it is Z."
- "Not merely X, but Y."
- "This is not about X; it is about Y."

State the positive directly. If the dismissed alternative matters, absorb it
as a trailing concession: "X is Z, not Y" — rarely, never as the default move.

**Filler phrases:**

- "it is worth noting," "it is important to remember"
- "truly," "deeply," "profoundly" (as emphasis)
- "delves into," "unpacks," "navigates"
- "at the intersection of," "in the realm of"
- "the fact that..." (almost always compressible)

**Performance words:** urgency, spectacle, hype. Anything that pushes rather
than invites. See `soul.md`: *"Invite, don't push."*

**Instructive constructions:** "you should," "you will find," "try to," "notice
how." The body is not addressed directly. The voice witnesses; it does not direct.

---

## When the prose feels off

The diagnostic: if a sentence has to be read twice, the structure isn't right
yet. If a claim needs three qualifiers to hold, the claim is larger than the
evidence supports — either earn it or trim it. If the opening announces what
the piece will do, delete the announcement and start with the substance.

When the prose feels off, slow down and find the structure it's missing. That
structure is what the prose was trying to reach.
